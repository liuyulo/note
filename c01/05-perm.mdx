## Odds and Evens

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>any permutation $\sigma\in S_n$ can be written as composition of 2-cycles</summary>

- $\sigma=\sigma_1\sigma_2\cdots\sigma_n=\pi_1\pi_2\cdots\pi_m\implies n$ and $m$ both even or both odd

<details open>
<summary>**Proof.**  let any $\sigma\in S_n$</summary>

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \sigma&=(a_1a_2\cdots a_n)\cdots(b_1b_2\cdots b_m)\\
  &=(a_1a_n)(a_1a_{n-1})\cdots(a_1a_2)\cdots(b_1b_m)(b_1b_{m-1})\cdots(b_1b_2)
\end{aligned}
$$
</details>

</details>
<details class="definition" open>
<summary></summary>
- permutation is **even** iff can decompose into even number of 2-cycles
- permutation is **odd** iff can decompose into odd number of 2-cycles
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>alternate group $A_n\le S_n$</summary>
set of even permutations

- $|A_n|=|S_n|/2=n!/2$
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>cycle notation of $S_n$</summary>
composition of $m$-cycles

- any permutation can be written as composition of cycles

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>disjoint cycles</summary>
cycles $\sigma$ and $\pi$ are disjoint iff have no entries in common
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>disjoint cycles commute</summary>
$(a_1a_2\cdots a_m)(b_1b_2\cdots b_n)=(b_1b_2\cdots b_n)(a_1a_2\cdots a_m)$
</details>

<details class="example">
<summary>composition to disjoint cycle form</summary>
let $\sigma=(13)(27)(456)$ and $\pi=(1237)(648)$

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \because&&1&\stackrel{(648)}\longrightarrow1\stackrel{(1237)}\longrightarrow2\stackrel{(456)}\longrightarrow2\stackrel{(27)}\longrightarrow7\stackrel{(13)}\longrightarrow7\\
  \therefore&&\sigma\pi&=(17\cdots)\cdots\\
  \because&&7&\stackrel{(648)}\longrightarrow7\stackrel{(1237)}\longrightarrow1\stackrel{(456)}\longrightarrow1\stackrel{(27)}\longrightarrow1\stackrel{(13)}\longrightarrow3\\
  \therefore&&\sigma\pi&=(173\cdots)\cdots\\
  \because&&\cdots&\\
  \therefore&&\sigma\pi&=(1732)(48)(56)
\end{aligned}
$$
</details>

## Permutation

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>permutation</summary>
a permutation of finite set $A$ is bijection $\sigma:A\to A$
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>symmetric group $S_n$</summary>
$S_n$ is set of all permutations of $[n]$

$$
\sigma=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&\cdots&n\\\sigma(1)&\sigma(2)&\cdots&\sigma(n)\\\end{pmatrix}\in S_n
$$

- $\varepsilon\in S_n$ is the identity
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>order of a permutation is the LCM of the cycle lengths</summary>
<details>
<summary>**Proof.** an $n$-cycle has order $n$</summary>
let $\sigma,\pi$ be disjoint cycles of length $m$ and $n$

- let $k=\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)$
- $\therefore \sigma^k=\pi^k=\varepsilon$
- $\therefore\varepsilon=\sigma^k\pi^k=(\sigma\pi)^k$ since $\sigma\pi=\pi\sigma$
- $\therefore$ let $t=|\sigma\pi|$, we have $t|k$, thus $t\le k$
- $\therefore \sigma^t\pi^t=(\sigma\pi)^t=\varepsilon$ since $t|t$
- $\therefore\sigma^t=\pi^t=\varepsilon$ since disjoint
- $\therefore t$ is common multiple of $m$ and $n$, thus $k\le t$
- $\therefore k=t$
</details>
</details>
