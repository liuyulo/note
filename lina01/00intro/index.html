Introduction

  • linguistic knowledge is unconscious
    • people speaking a language can explain what constructions are correct or incorrect in that language, but can’the explain why
    • unconscious knowledge = competence
  • register/style is to level of formality
    • e.g. informal register, formal register

(Mental) Grammar

mental system of rules that exists in the head of native speakers - phonetics: perception and articulation of speech sounds - phonology: sound combinations (which combso are possible) - morphology: sentence structure - semantics: meaning and interpretation of sentences

Prescriptive Grammar

believe in absolute standard of correctness, what speakers should or shouldn’the do - one form is more logical than another - appeal to classical forms - preference for older forms of the language - injunction against the use of foreign words

Descriptive Grammar

studies and characterises the language of specific groups of people in a range of situations, what speakers do or don’t do - linguists are descriptive grammarians - all varieties of a language are valid systems with their logic and conventions - no scientific reason to expect one language to match another - languages are continually changing without reducing their usefullness, preciseness or aesthetic value - all languages have adopted words from other sources

Characteristics of Grammar

  • generality: individual grammatical systems differ in the use of structure formation rules
  • parity: all grammers are equal and (unconsciously) instruct people on how to form and interpret words and sentences of their language
  • mutability: grammar change overer time
  • inaccessibiliy: grammatical knowledge is unconscious
  • universality: all languages share an inventory of principles and properties

Standard

standard dialects are usually based on the speech of political elite or upper class
