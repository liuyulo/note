deductive validity and inductive strength difference
  • ∵ deductive reasoning is using one or more premises to reach a logical conclusion
  • inductive reasoning is making inferences using a sample of a population
  • ∴ a deductively valid argument can give absolute guarantee
  • the strength of an inductive argument only makes the conclusion more or less plausible and does not provide absolute guarantee
logic of confirmation vs disconformation
  • the logic of confirmation is to use an observation to confirm what a theory predicts
  • the logic of disconfirmation is to use an observation that doesnt follow what the theory predicts to falsify it
  • the logic of disconfirmation is deductively valid because the counterexample creates a contradiction, which implies that the theory is false.
argument definition & structure
  • a structure of statement that is designed to prove a point
  • consists of premises and a conclusion
if design argument is inductive, then it’s weak
  • if show the universe is the product of design using induction
  • then need to make this conclusion by observing a large number of universes in order to have a large sample
  • but we don’t have other universes to observe
  • ∴ the sample size is zero
  • ∴ this inductive argument is weak.

2 Deductive Reasoning

use one or more premises to reach a logical conclusion

good arguments are

  • rationally persuasive
  • have true premises: a sound argument
  • premise and conclusion are related
  • deductively valid ⊕ abductively strong ⊕ inductively strong

Validity

premise is true => conclusion must be true

  • has a logical form
  • doesn’t have to have true premises: can have implausible statements
  • ONLY ARGUMENTS CAN BE VALID

Limitation

  • deductive validity gives absolute guarantee ∴ very conservative
  • but prohibits from reaching conclusions beyond the information given in the premises
  • nondeductive inference gives a weaker guarantee, but it makes the conclusion plausible ∴ has risks

Circularity (Fallacy)

An premise that is assumed to be true occurs again in the conclusion

  • e.g. my fav color is black ∵ it’s black

Surprise Principle

O favors H1 over H2 only if when H1 is true, O is expected, and when H2 is true, O is not expected
aka relative probability

suppose observation O and hypothesis H1 and H2
  • H1 -> O expected
  • H2 -> ¬O expected
  • O is strongly favor H1 voer H2

example: H1 = has heartattack, H2 = ¬H1

  • bad O = has a heart
    • H1 -> O expected
    • H2 -> O expected
    • O is not a strong evidence of H1
  • good O = erratic ECG
    • H1 -> O expected
    • H2 -> O not expected
    • O is a strong evidence of H1

SP Discrimination

always think about alternative hypetheses for an observation

example: O = buys philosophy books

  • H1: is PHL major (expect true)
  • H2: is engineering major (expect false)
  • H3: not PHL major, but interested in PHL (expect true)

dosen’t discriminate between H1 and H3

“Only Game in Town Fallacy”

the fact that you can’t think of a more plausible explanation of the observation doesnt oblige you to accept the other explanation

9 Testability of God’s Existence

Testability Theory of Meaning

if S is a meaningful sentence, then it is possible to figure out whether S is true

i.e. if S is a priori or a posteriori, then S is meaningful

  • positivists: “God ∃” is neither a priori nor a posteriori, ∴ the claim is meaningless

Analytical vs Synthetic Truth

  • analytical truth are decidable from definition alone (truth is contained within the definition of the terms in it)
    • e.g. people die if they are killed, people are born on their birthday
    • positivists: all a priori truth are analytical
  • synthetic truth is not analytical truth
    • e.g. I got 4.0 in PHLA10
      • positivists: all a posteriori truth are synthetic
  • positivists:
    • “God ∃” cant be decided from definition alone ∴ not analytical
    • “God ∃” makes no predictions, ∴ not testable ∴ not synthetic
    • if a theory make a prediction that can be checked observationally, then it runs the risk of being refuted by observations

10 Pascal and Irrationality

Prudential and Evidential Reasons

  • belief with prudential reasons: believe because self-interest
  • belief with evidential reasons: believe because evidences exist

Pascal tries to give a prudential reason for believing in God (as long as you think that the existence of God is possible)

Pascal’s Argument (and Criticisms)

suppose the following outcomes of “belive God ∃” vs “God actually ∃”

God ∃ God ∄
Believe God ∃ +infinite -finite
Believe God ∄ -infinite +finite

Pascal:

  • all possible outcomes:
    • believe in God while God ∃ will get infinite reward (e.g. goto heaven)
    • believe in God while God ∄ will get finite punishment (e.g. waste time during religious practices)
    • not believe in God while God ∃ will get infinite punishment (e.g. goto hell)
    • not believe in God while God ∄ will get finite punishment (e.g. not waste time)
  • ∴ by reasonable gambling, the expected value is higher for believe in God no matter how low the chances are
    • ∴ its better to believe than not

Criticism: Belief ∉ Action

  • we cant decide to believe a proposition ∵ it isnt an action we can control
  • objection: treat belief as a problem of decision theory is a mistake
  • Pascal: reformulate argument into “decide doing things under control that leads to believe in God”

Criticism: Assumption of Outcome

  • Pascal assumes God would send believers to heaven and nonbelievers to hell
    • i.e. assumes God’s nature that not all religious traditions necessary have
    • i.e. assumes what God would be like if God ∃

11 Argument from Evil

evil ∃ => God ∄

Logical: No Evil

  • God ∃ => God is omniscience, omnipotence, omnibenevolence
  • ∃ being that is omni => ∄ evil
  • ∃ evil
  • God ∄

Explanation

  1. definition of God
  2. an omnibenevolence (all-good) God would want to prevent evil
    • an omniscience (all-knowing) God would know how to prevent evil
    • an omnipontent (all-powerful) God would be able to prevent evil
    • ∴ an all-pkg will prevent the existence of evil
  3. two kinds of evil
    • by human actions (e.g. conflict, wars, etc)
    • by nature that makes human suffer (e.g. natural disasters)

Problems

  1. theodicy and defence
    • theodicy (explain why it’s false)
      • why an omni God would allow evil to exist
    • defence (no good reason to think the premise is true)
  2. assumes evil exists
    • assumes murders/tortures/brutalites is morally bad
    • assumes world would be a better place if some natural disaters didnt occur

Theodicy: Soul-Building Evils

  • soul-building: evils that make us better people, strengthen our character
    • not all evils/suffers make a person stronger
    • some suffers weaken a person, but make other people stronger
  • assume (1) and (3) are true
    • why couldnt a omnipontent God make us stronger directly?
    • “to let us feel achievement, get credit”
    • why need to get credit?
    • why get credit for enduring suffer > just become stronger by God?

Logical: No Above Soul-Building

  • God ∃ => God is omni
  • ∃ omni being => amount of evil <= soul-building minimum
  • amount of evil > a soul-building min
  • God ∄

Theodicy: Free Will

  1. God made us free, ∴ ∃ people who do evil without soul-building
    • why God dont make us free and only do what is right
    • why free => ∃ people who perform evil actions?

Logical: No Above SB + Freedom

  • God ∃ => God is omni
  • ∃ omni being => amount of evil <= sould-building min + consequence of freedom
  • amount of evil exceeds
  • God ∄

Defence: Amount of Evil

  1. attempts to specify an upper bound of evilness
    • how measure?
    • God can allow more evil to get some greater good of which we are unaware
