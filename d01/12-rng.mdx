<details class="definition" open>
<summary>ring</summary>

a **ring** $R$ is set with *addition* and *multiplication* such that

- $R$ is abelian additive group
- $R$ is multiplicative semigroup
- multiplication is distributive wrt addition

trivial ring is $\{0\}$

</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary></summary>

- ring **with unity** is *non-trivial* ring with multiplicative identity (i.e. multiplicative monoid)
- **commutative** ring is ring with commutative multiplication 
- $a$ **divides** $b\iff a|b$, that is $\exists c\enspace b=ac$
- let $R^*$ denote $R\setminus\{0\}$ (without add iden)
- $a\in R^*$ is a **unit** $\iff a$ has multiplicative inverse
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>characteristic</summary>

for ring $R,r\in R$ and $n\in\mathbb Z^+$, let $nr$ denote

$$
\underbrace{r+\cdots+r}_n
$$

the **characteristic** of ring $R$ is 

$$
\operatorname{char}R=\min\{n\in\mathbb Z^+:nr=0\enspace r\in R\}
$$

or $\operatorname{char}R=0$ if no such $n$ exists
</details>


<details class="example" open>
<summary>everyday rings</summary>

- $M_n(R)$ is noncomm ring of nxn matrices over ring $R$
- $\mathbb Z[i]$ is ring of gaussian integers
- $\mathbb Z_n$ is comm finite ring with nonzero char
- $\mathbb Z_n[x]$ is comm infinite ring with nonzero char
- $R_1\oplus R_2$ is ring
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>subring test</summary>

for ring $R$ and nonempty $S\sube R$ where

- $S$ closed under difference
- $S$ closed under multiplication

then $S$ is subring of $R$

</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>center of ring</summary>

for ring $R$, its **center** is 

$$
Z(R)=\{r:\forall a\in R\enspace ar=ra\}
$$

</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>commutative rings</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** outer/inner cancellation implies commutative</summary>

let any ring $R$

assume $ab=ca\implies  b=c$ for any $a\ne0$, wts $R$ commutative

let any $a,b\in R$

case $b=0$

$\therefore ab=a0=0=0a=ab$

case $b\ne 0$

$\because b(ab)=bab=(ba)b$

$\therefore ab=ba\enspace\square$
</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** every $x^2-x$ commutes implies commutative</summary>

let any $a\in R$

- wts $a^2\in Z(R)$, let any $b\in R$
  $$
  \begin{align*}
      \because&&(a^2-a)+(b^2-b)+ab+ba&=(a+b)^2-(a+b)\in Z(R)\\
      \therefore&& ab+ba&\in Z(R)\tag{$Z(R)$ is ring}\\
      \therefore&& a^2b+aba&=a(ab+ba)=(ab+ba)a=aba+ba^2=ba^2+aba\\
      \therefore&& a^2b&=ba^2\\
  \end{align*}
  $$
- thus $a^2\in Z(R)$ as wanted
- wts $a\in Z(R)$
  $$
  \begin{align*}
    \because&& a^2-a,a^2\in Z(R)\\
    \therefore&& a=a^2-(a^2-a)\in Z(R)\tag{$Z(R)$ is ring}
  \end{align*}
  $$
thus $R\subseteq Z(R)$

$\therefore R=Z(R)$ is commutative $\enspace\square$

</details>


<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** every $x^2+x$ commutes implies commutative</summary>

let any $r\in R$

$$
\therefore r^2-r=(-r)^2+(-r)\in Z(R)
$$

</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** every $[x^2,y]=[x,y]$ implies commutative</summary>

$$
\begin{align*}
  \because&& x^2y-yx^2&=xy-yx\\
  \therefore&&(x^2-x)y&=y(x^2-x)\\
  \therefore&& x^2-x&\in Z(R)\enspace\square
\end{align*}
$$


</details>

<details>
<summary>**Proof.** every $x^2=x$ implies commutative</summary>

let any $a,b\in R$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \therefore&& -a&=(-a)^2=a^2\\
  \therefore&&ab&=ab+(a\textcolor{red}{-a})+(b\textcolor{red}{-b})+(ab-ab)+(ba-ba)\\
  &&&=ab+\textcolor{red}{a^2}+ab+ba+\textcolor{red}{b^2}-a-b-ab-ba\\
  &&&=ab+(a+b)^2-a-b-ab-ba\\
  &&&=ab+a+b-a-b-ab-ba=-ba=ba\enspace\square
\end{align*}
$$

</details>
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>Boolean rings: every element is idempotent</summary>


<details>
<summary>**Proof.** powerset is boolean ring</summary>

for any nonempty $X$, let $\mathcal{P}(X)$ be bool ring with

$$
\begin{align*}
  A+B&=A\triangle B\\
  A\cdot B&=A\cap B\\
  0&=\varnothing\\
  1&=X\\
\end{align*}
$$

</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** for commutative ring, unit + nilpotent is unit</summary>

let any comm ring $R$

assume $a\in R$ is unit, and $b\in R$ is nilpotent: $b^2=0$

wts $a+b$ is unit

$$
\begin{align*}
  (a+b)\cdot a^{-1}(1-a^{-1}b)=(1+a^{-1}b)(1-a^{-1}b)=1-a^{-2}b^2=1\enspace\square
\end{align*}
$$
</details>


</details>

## Integral Domain

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>integral domain</summary>

an **integral domain** $D$ is a comm ring with unity that has no zero divisors, that is,
$$
\forall a\forall b\enspace ab=0\implies a\ne 0\implies b=0
$$

nonzero $a$ in comm ring $R$ is a **zero divisor** $\iff\exists b\in R^*$ such that $ab=0$

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>integral domain iff cancellation</summary>
$R$ is integral domain iff cancellation, that is

$$
\forall a\in R^*\enspace\forall b, c\in R\enspace ab=ac\implies b=c
$$

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
$(\implies)$ assume intdom

- let any $ab=ac$ where $a\ne0$
- $\therefore 0=ab-ac=a(b-c)$
- $\therefore b-c=0$, thus $b=c$
- $\therefore$ cancellation

$(\impliedby)$ assume cancellation

- let any $ab=0$ and $a\ne 0$
- $\because ab=a0$ and $a\ne 0$
- $\therefore b=0$
- $\therefore$ intdom
</details>
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>for finite commutative ring</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** every nonzero element is either zero-divisor or unit</summary>

let any $a\in R^*$, assume not zero-divisor, wts is unit

$\therefore f=b\mapsto ab$ is $f:R^*\to R^*$

BWOC assume $f$ not surj

- $\therefore f$ not inj <span class="float-right">($R$ finite)</span>
- $\therefore\exists b\ne c\in R^*$ st $ab=ac$
- $\therefore a(b-c)=ab-ac=0$ where $b-c\ne 0$, contradicts that $a$ is not zero-div

$\therefore f:R^*\twoheadrightarrow R^*$ where $1\in R^*$

$\therefore\exists b\in R^*$ st $ab=1\enspace\square$

</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** has a non-zero-divisor iff has unity</summary>

$(\impliedby )1$ is a not zero divisor

$(\implies )$ let $a$ be non-zero divisor and $|R|=n$

<details>
<summary>$\therefore T_a:R\stackrel\sim\to R$ whene $T_a(b)=ab$</summary>

- otherwise $T_a$ not injective
- $\therefore\exists b\ne c$ st $ab=ac$
- $\therefore a(b-c)=0$ where $b-c\ne 0$, contradicts that $a$ is not zero-div
</details>

$\therefore\langle T_a\rangle\le S_n$ is finite, let $k=|\langle T_a\rangle|$

$\therefore T_a^k=T_{a^k}$ is $1_R$, that is, identity mapping

$\therefore a^k$ is multiplicative identity (aka unity) $\square$

</details>

</details>


<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$\mathbb Z_n$ is integral domain $\iff n$ is prime</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
$(\implies)$ assume $n$ is composite

- $\therefore\exists1\lt a,b\lt n$ such that $n=ab$
- $\therefore ab\equiv 0\mod n$, thus $a\in\mathbb Z_n$ is zero divisor
- $\therefore\mathbb Z_n$ is not intdom

$(\impliedby)$ assume $n$ is prime

- let any $a,b\in\mathbb Z_n$ st $ab=0$
- $\therefore p|ab$
- $\therefore p|a$ or $p|b$ since $p$ is prime
- $\therefore a=0$ or $b=0$
- $\therefore \mathbb Z_n$ is intdom $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>characteristic of integral domain is $0$ or prime</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
assume $\operatorname{char} D=n$, wts $n$ is prime

let any $1\le s,t\le n$ such that $n=st$

- $\therefore0=n\cdot 1=st\cdot1=(s\cdot 1)(t\cdot 1)$
- $\therefore s\cdot 1=0$ or $t\cdot 1=0$
- $\because n$ is smallest integer st $n\cdot 1=0$
- $\therefore s=n$ or $t=n$, thus $n$ prime $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>commutative ring with unique aba is integral domain</summary>

for commutative ring $R$ with at least two elements where

$$
\forall a\in R^*\quad\exists!b\in R\quad aba=a
$$

then $R$ is integral domain

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

(1) wts no divisors

let any $a\in R^*$ and any $c\in R$, assume $ac=0$, wts $c=0$

$\because\exists!b\in R\quad aba=a$

$\therefore aba=aba+0a=aba+aca=a(b+c)a$

$\therefore b=b+c$, thus $c=b-b=0$ as  wanted

thus cancellation holds

(2) wts has unity

choose some $a\in R^*$ <span class="float-right">($|R|\ge2$)</span>

$\therefore\exists!b\in R\quad aba=a$

wts $ab$ is unity, let any $r\in R$

$\because raba=ra$ where $a\in R^*$

$\therefore r(ab)=r$, and $(ab)r=r$ by comm $\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>no integral domain has 6 elements</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** let any commutative ring $R$ with 6 elements</summary>

wts $R$ as a zero divisor

consider the additive group $(R,+)$ of order 6

$\therefore R$ has some element $a$ with order 2, and $b$ with order 3<span class="float-right">(Sylow 1)</span>

$\therefore$ let $c=a+b$, thus $c$ has additive order 6

$\therefore 2c,3c\ne 0$ and $6c=0$ 

however in $R$ as a comm ring

$$
(2c)(3c)=(6c)c=0c=0
$$

$\therefore 2c$ is zero divisor of $R$, thus not integral domain $\enspace\square$

</details>
</details>



## Field

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>field</summary>
- **field** $F$ is commutative ring where $F^*$ is multiplicative group
- **field** $F$ is commutative ring where every nonzero element is unit
- **field** $F$ is ring where $F^*$ is abelian multiplicative group
</details>


<details class="theorem" open>
<summary></summary>

<details>
<summary>**Proof.** any field is an integral domain</summary>
let any $ab=0$ and $a\ne 0$

- $\therefore b=a^{-1}ab=a^{-1}0=0$
- $\therefore$ is intdom
</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** any finite integral domain is field</summary>
let any finite intdom $D$, wts has inverse

- let $D=\{0,1=a_0,a_1,\cdots,a_n\}$ and let $A=\{a_i\}\sub D$
- let any $a\in D^*$, consider $aA\sube D$
- case $\exists i\ne j$ st $aa_i=aa_j$
- $\therefore a_i=a_j$, contradicts that $a_0,\cdots,a_n$ are distinct
- $\because 0\in aA$, otherwise $D$ would have a zero divisor
- $\therefore aA=A$
- $\therefore 1\in aA$
- $\therefore a$ has a inverse in $A\sub D\quad\square$
</details>

**Corollary.** $\mathbb Z_p$ is field for any prime $p$
</details>
