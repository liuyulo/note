## Cauchy

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>$(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{T}_E)$ cauchy</summary>

sequence $(a_n)$ is **cauchy** iff

$$
\forall \varepsilon\gt0\quad\exists N\gt0\quad\forall i,j\gt N\quad|a_i-a_j|\lt\varepsilon
$$

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{T}_E)$ cauchy implies bounded</summary>
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

assume $(a_n)$ cauchy

$\therefore\exists N\gt0\quad\forall i,j\gt N\quad |a_i-a_j|\lt 1$

$\therefore$ for any $i\gt N$

$$
|a_i|\le|a_i-a_{N+1}|+|a_{N+1}|\lt 1+|a_{N+1}|
$$

let $r=\max\{|a_1|,\cdots,|a_N|,1+|a_{N+1}|\}$

$\therefore |a_n|\le r$ for any $n\gt0\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>

## Convergence

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>convergence</summary>

for space $(X,\mathcal{T})$

- sequence $(a_n)\subseteq X$ **coverges** to $\alpha\in X$, denoted $a_n\to\alpha$ iff

$$
\forall\alpha\in U\in\mathcal{T} \quad\exists N\gt0\quad\forall n\gt N\quad a_n\in U
$$

- that is, only finite many points are outside any arbitrary neighborhood of $\alpha$

for metric space $(X,d)$

- sequence $(a_n)\in X$ **converges** to $\alpha\in X$ iff

$$
\forall\varepsilon\gt0\quad\exists N\gt0\quad\forall n\gt N\quad a_n\in B_d(\alpha,\varepsilon)
$$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$(T_2)$ any sequence converge to at most one point</summary>


<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

assume $a_n\to\alpha$ and $a_n\to\beta$, BWOC assume $\alpha\ne\beta$

- $\therefore$ let $U_\alpha$ and $U_\beta$ be neighborhoods that separate $\alpha$ and $\beta$ respectively
- $\therefore(a_n)$ almost contained in $U_\alpha$<span class="float-right">($a_n\to\alpha$)</span>

let $A=\{a_n\}$
- $\therefore A\cap U_\beta$ is subset of $A\cap U_\alpha^c$ which is finite
- $\therefore$ contradicts that $(a_n)$ almost contained in $U_\beta\enspace\square$<span class="float-right">($a_n\to\beta$)</span>

</details>

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$(X,d)$ any sequence converges to at most one limit</summary>

<details>
<summary>**Lemma.** $\forall\varepsilon\gt0\quad d(x,y)\lt\varepsilon\implies  x=y$</summary>

BWOC assume $x\ne y$, thus $d(x,y)\gt0$

- $\therefore$ contradicts when $\varepsilon=d(x,y)\enspace\square$
</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

assume $a_n\to x$ and $a_n\to y$, wts $x=y$

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

- $\therefore d(a_n,x),d(a_n,y)\lt\varepsilon/2$ where $n=N+1$ for some $N\gt0$

- $\therefore d(x,y)\le d(x,a_n)+d(a_n,y)\lt\varepsilon/2+\varepsilon/2=\varepsilon$

$\therefore x=y$ by lemma
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$(X,d)$ convergent implies bounded</summary>

for any sequence $(a_n)\subseteq  X$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$(a_n)$ converges $\implies  (a_n)$ bounded
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

assume $a_n\to\alpha$

$\therefore\exists N\quad\forall n\gt N\quad d(a_n,\alpha)\lt1$

let $r=\max\{d(a_1,\alpha),\cdots,d(a_N,\alpha),1\}$

$\therefore \{a_n\}\subseteq B(\alpha,r)$, thus $(a_n)$ bounded $\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{T}_E)$ cauchy iff convergent</summary>

for any sequence $(a_n)\subseteq\mathbb{R}$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$(a_n)$ cauchy $\iff (a_n)$ converges
</div>


<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** $(\impliedby) $</summary>

assume $a_n\to \alpha$

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

$$
\therefore\exists N\gt0\quad\forall n\gt N\quad|a_n-\alpha|\lt\varepsilon/2
$$

let any $i,j\gt N$


$$
\therefore|a_i-a_j|\le|a_i-\alpha|+|a_j-\alpha|\lt\varepsilon/2+\varepsilon/2=\varepsilon
$$
</details>
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** $(\implies )$ </summary>

assume $(a_n)$ cauchy, thus $A=\{a_n\}$ bounded

WLOG assume $A$ is infinite

$\therefore A$ has some accumulation point $\alpha$<span class="float-right">(BW)</span>

$\therefore\alpha$ is limit point of $A$<span class="float-right">($T_1$)</span>

<details open>
<summary>wts $a_n\to\alpha$</summary>

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

$$
\therefore\exists N\gt0\quad\forall i,j\gt N\quad|a_i-a_j|\lt\varepsilon/2
$$

$\because|B(\alpha,\varepsilon/2)\cap A|\ge\aleph_0$<span class="float-right">($\alpha$ is limit point)</span>

$\therefore\exists a_m\in B(\alpha,\varepsilon/2)\cap\{a_{N+1},a_{N+2},\cdots\}$, that is, $m\gt N$

let any $n\gt N$

$$
\therefore |a_n-\alpha|\le\underbrace{|a_n-a_m|}_{n,m\gt N}+\underbrace{|\alpha-a_m|}_{a_m\in B(\alpha,\varepsilon/2)}\lt\varepsilon/2+\varepsilon/2=\varepsilon\enspace\square
$$
</details>

</details>

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary></summary>

let $a_n\to \alpha\quad b_n\to \beta\quad c_n\to \gamma$

$$
\begin{align*}
  a_n+b_n&\to \alpha+\beta\\
  a_nb_n&\to \alpha\beta\\
  ca_n&\to c\alpha\\
\end{align*}
\\
\begin{align*}
  \forall n&& a_n\le b_n\le c_n\text{ and }\alpha=\gamma&\implies \alpha=\beta=\gamma\\
  \forall n&& a_n\le b_n&\implies \alpha\le\beta
\end{align*}
$$

<details class="list-none" open>
<summary>**Proof.** $a_n\le b_n\implies \alpha\le\beta$</summary>

BWOC assume $\alpha\gt\beta$

$\therefore\exists q\in\mathbb{R}$ such that $\beta\lt q\lt \alpha$<span class="float-right">(density)</span>

$\because(-\infty,q)$ is neighborhood of $\beta$, and $(q,\infty)$ is neighborhood of $\alpha$

$\therefore\exists a_n\exists b_n$ such thath $b_n\lt q\lt a_n$

thus contradicts that $a_n\le b_n\enspace\square$

</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>M ⊢ B `<=>` C</summary>

for monotone $(a_n)$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$(a_n)$ bounded iff $(a_n)$ is converges
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Lemma<sup>+</sup>** increases and bounded above `==>` converges to supremum</summary>

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

- $\therefore\exists N\quad\alpha-\varepsilon\lt a_N$<span class="float-right">($\alpha$ is sup)</span>
- $\therefore$ for any $n\gt N$

$$
\alpha-\varepsilon\lt a_N\le a_n\le\alpha\lt \alpha+\varepsilon
$$

- $\therefore|a_n-\alpha|\lt\varepsilon$

</details>

- **Lemma<sup>-</sup>** decreases and bounded below `==>` converges to infimum

<details>
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

$(\implies)$ trivial\
$(\impliedby)$ by lemma <sup>+</sup> and <sup>-</sup>
</details>
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>next term close to convergent series implies converges</summary>

for any sequence $(\varepsilon_n\gt0)$ whose series $s_n\to s$

for any sequence $(a_n)$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$\forall n|a_n-a_{n+1}|\lt\varepsilon_n\implies (a_n)$ converges
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

- $\therefore\exists N\quad\forall n\ge N\quad|s-s_n|\lt\varepsilon$

- $\therefore$ for any $m\gt n\gt N$

$$
\begin{align*}
  |a_n-a_m|&\le|a_n+a_{n+1}|+\cdots +|a_{m-1}-a_m|\\
  &\le\varepsilon_n+\varepsilon_{n+1}+\cdots \varepsilon_{m-1}\\
  &\le\varepsilon_n+\varepsilon_{n+1}+\cdots\\
  &=s-s_{n+1}=|s-s_{n+1}|\lt\varepsilon
\end{align*}
$$

$\therefore(a_n)$ cauchy, thus converges $\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>


## Subsequence

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>subsequence</summary>

for sequence $(a_n)$ and indices $0\lt n_1\lt n_2\lt\cdots$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$(a_{n_k})$ is a subsequence
</div>

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>(BW corollary) bounded implies has convergent subsequence</summary>

for any sequence $(a_n)\subseteq\mathbb{R}$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$(a_n)$ bounded $\implies (a_n)$ has convergent subsequence
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

let any bounded sequence $(a_n)$

WLOG assume $A=\{a_n\}$ infinite

$\therefore A$ has some accumulation point $\alpha$<span class="float-right">(BW)</span>

$\therefore\alpha$ is limit point of $A$<span class="float-right">($T_1$)</span>

$\therefore$ for any $k\in\mathbb{Z}^+$

$$
|B(\alpha,1/k)\cap A|\ge\aleph_0
$$

let subsequence $(a_{n_k})\subseteq(a_n)$ be defined inductively

- choose $a_{n_1}\in B(\alpha,1)\cap A$<span class="float-right">(interesection is infinite)</span>
- for any $k\in\mathbb{Z}^+$, choose $n_{k+1}\gt n_k$ st
  $$
  a_{n_{k+1}}\in B\left(\alpha,\frac1{k+1}\right)\cap\{a_{n_k+1},a_{n_k+2},\cdots\}
  $$
  - where this intersection is also infinite

$\therefore n_1\lt n_2\lt\cdots$ and $a_{n_k}\in B(\alpha,1/k)$ for any $k$

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

- $\therefore\exists N\in\mathbb{Z}^+\quad 1/N\lt\varepsilon$<span class="float-right">(arch)</span>
- $\therefore$ for any $k\gt N$
  $$
  |a_{n_k}-\alpha|\lt 1/k\lt 1/N\lt \varepsilon\enspace\square
  $$

</details>
</details>


<details class="example" open>
<summary>convergent (?) subsequences in $\mathbb{R}$</summary>

- $a_n=(-1)^n/n\implies a_{2n}\to0$

<details>
<summary>BWOC assume $a_n=n$ has some convergent $(a_{n_k})$</summary>

$\therefore(a_{n_k})$ bounded by some $M$

$\because\exists k$ st $n_k\gt M$

$\therefore a_{n_k}=n_k\gt M$, contradicts that $(a_{n_k})$ bounded $\enspace\square$

</details>

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>sequence converges iff all subsequences converge</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>


$(\impliedby )$ a sequence is a subsequence of itself

$(\implies )$ assume $a_n\to\alpha$, let any subsequence $(a_{n_k})$

- let any $\varepsilon\gt0$, thus

$$
\exists N\gt0\quad\forall n\gt N\quad|a_n-\alpha|\lt\varepsilon
$$

- $\therefore$ for any $n_k\gt N$
$$
|a_{n_k}-\alpha|\lt\varepsilon\enspace\square
$$
</details>

</details>


<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>any sequence has a monotone subsequence</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

let any sequence $(a_n)$

let $P=\{a_n:\forall k\ge n\quad a_n\ge a_k\}$ be set of **peaks**

case $P$ infinite

- $\therefore$ let $(a_{n_k})$ be sequence of $P$ ordered by index, thus $(a_{n_k})$ decreases

case $P$ finite

- $\therefore \exists N$ st $a_n$ is not peak for any $n\ge N$

let $(a_{n_k})$ be defined inductively:

- let $n_1=N$, thus $a_{n_1}$ is not peak
- for any $k\ge1$, assume $n_k\ge N$ and $a_{n_k}$ is not peak
  - $\therefore\exists m\gt n_k$ st $a_{n_k}\lt a_m$<span class="float-right">(def of peak)</span>

  - $\therefore$ let $n_{k+1}=m\gt n_k\ge N$, thus $a_{n_{k+1}}$ is not peak

- $\therefore (a_{n_k})$ is increases $\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>


<details class="example" open>
<summary></summary>

for bounded sequence $(a_n)$ 

<div class="flex justify-center">

any convergent subseq converges to the same $\alpha\implies (a_n)$ also converges to $\alpha$
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

BWOC assume $(a_n)$ doesn't converge to $\alpha$

$\therefore\exists\varepsilon\gt0\quad\forall k\gt0\quad\exists n_k\gt k$ such that
$$
|a_{n_k}-\alpha|\ge\varepsilon
$$

$\therefore$ we can choose such positive sequence $(n_k)$ that strictly increases

let $(b_k)=(a_{n_k})$ be subsequence of $(a_n)$, thus

$$
\forall k\gt0\quad |b_k-\alpha|\ge\varepsilon
$$

$\because(b_k)$ bounded since $(a_n)$ bounded

$\therefore(b_k)$ has some convergent subsequence $(c_i)=(b_{k_i})$<span class="float-right">(BW)</span>

$\therefore$ for any $i\gt0$

$$
|c_i-\alpha|\ge\varepsilon
$$

which contradicts that $c_i\to\alpha\enspace\square$<span class="float-right">($c_i$ is convergent subsequence of $a_n$)</span>
</details>
</details>

## Limit Superior & Limit Inferior


<details class="definition" open>
<summary>limsup liminf</summary>

for any bounded $(a_n)\subseteq\mathbb{R}$, let


$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
A&=\{a_n\}\\
A_{\lt\alpha}&=\{a_n\in A:a_n\lt\alpha\}\\
A_\lt&=\{\alpha\in\mathbb{R}:|A_{\lt\alpha}|\lt\aleph_0\}
\end{split}\quad
\begin{split}
A_n&=\{a_n,a_{n+1},\cdots \}\\
A_{\gt\alpha}&=\{a_n\in A:a_n\gt\alpha\}\\
A_\gt&=\{\alpha\in \mathbb{R}:|A_{\gt\alpha}|\lt\aleph_0\}\\
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
$$

**limit superior**: TFAE, denoted as $\displaystyle \varlimsup_{n\to\infty} a_n=\overline\alpha$

- ①  $\overline\alpha=\inf A_>$
  - infimum of $\alpha$s such that only finite many elements are above $\alpha$

- ② $\forall\varepsilon\gt0\quad|A_{\gt\overline\alpha+\varepsilon}|\lt\aleph_0\quad|A_{\gt\overline\alpha-\varepsilon}|\ge\aleph_0$
  - finitely many elements above $\overline\alpha+\varepsilon$
  - infintely many elments above $\overline\alpha-\varepsilon$

- ③ $\sup A_n\to\overline\alpha$

  - aka $\inf\{\sup A_n\}=\overline\alpha$ since $(\sup A_n)$ decreases

- ④ $\overline\alpha$ is supremum of $(a_n)$'s subsequential limits

**limit inferior**: TFAE, denoted as $\displaystyle\varliminf_{n\to\infty}a_n=\underline\alpha$

- ① $\underline\alpha=\sup A_\lt$
  - supremum of $\alpha$s such that only finitely many elements are below $\alpha$

- ② $\forall\varepsilon\gt0\quad|A_{\lt\underline\alpha-\varepsilon}|\lt\aleph_0\quad|A_{\lt\underline\alpha+\varepsilon}|\ge\aleph_0$

  - finitely many elements below $\underline\alpha-\varepsilon$
  - infinitely many elements below $\underline\alpha+\varepsilon$

- ③ $\inf A_n\to\underline\alpha$

  - aka $\sup\{\inf A_n\}=\underline\alpha$ since $(\inf A_n)$ increases

- ④ $\alpha$ is infimum of $(a_n)$'s subsequential limits
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>(limsup) ①  `=>` ②  `=>` ③  `=>` ④  `=>` ① </summary>

<details>
<summary>**Proof.** assume ① , wts ② </summary>

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

$\therefore\exists\alpha\in A_\gt$ such that

$$
|A_{\gt\alpha}|\lt\aleph_0\qquad\overline\alpha\le\alpha\lt\overline\alpha+\varepsilon
$$

$\therefore A_{\gt\overline\alpha+\varepsilon}\subseteq A_{\gt\alpha}$, thus $|A_{\gt\overline\alpha+\varepsilon}|\lt\aleph_0$

$\because\overline\alpha-\varepsilon\notin A_>$<span class="float-right">($\overline\alpha$ is lower bound)</span>

$\therefore|A_{\gt\overline\alpha-\varepsilon}|\ge\aleph_0\enspace\square$
</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** assume ② , wts ③ </summary>

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

(1) wts $\sup A_n\lt\overline\alpha+\varepsilon$

- $\because|A_{\gt\alpha+\varepsilon/2}|\lt\aleph_0$
- $\therefore\exists N\quad\forall n\gt N\quad a_n\le\overline\alpha+\varepsilon/2$
- $\therefore$ for any $n\gt N$

$$
\sup A_n\le\overline\alpha+\varepsilon/2\lt\alpha+\varepsilon
$$

(2) wts $\sup A_n\gt\overline\alpha-\varepsilon$

- $\because|A_{\gt\overline\alpha-\varepsilon}|\ge\aleph_0$
- $\therefore\forall n\gt N\quad\exists n'\ge n\quad a_{n'}\gt\overline\alpha-\varepsilon$
- $\therefore$ for any $n\gt N$

$$
\sup A_n\ge a_{n'}\gt\overline\alpha-\varepsilon
$$

thus with (1) (2), for any $n\gt N$

$$
|\sup A_n-\overline\alpha|\lt\varepsilon
$$

thus $\sup A_n\to\overline\alpha\enspace\square$
</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** assume ③ , wts ④ </summary>

let $S$ be set of subsequential limits

$$
S=\{\alpha\in\mathbb{R}:\exists(a_{n_k})\subseteq(a_n)\quad a_{n_k}\to\alpha\}
$$

wts $\sup S\le\overline\alpha$

- let any $(a_{n_k})$ that converges to some $\alpha\in S$
  - let any $k\gt0$, thus $n_k\ge k$
  $$
  \begin{align*}
  \because&&a_{n_k}&\in A_{n_k}\subseteq A_k\\
  \therefore&&a_{n_k}&\le\sup A_{n_k}\le\sup A_k
  \end{align*}
  $$
  - where $a_{n_k}\to\alpha$ and $\sup A_k\to\overline\alpha$

- $\therefore\alpha\le\overline\alpha$

wts $\overline\alpha\in S$, thus $\overline\alpha=\max S=\sup S$

- for any $k\gt0$, let $\varepsilon_k=1/k\gt0$

  $$
  \therefore\exists n_k\ge k\quad \sup A_k-\varepsilon_k\le a_{n_k}\le \sup A_k
  $$
- choose the smallest $n_k$ for any $k$ thus $(a_{n_k})$ is a subsequence of $(a_n)$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \therefore&&\lim_{k\to\infty}(\sup A_k-\varepsilon_k)&\le\lim_{k\to\infty}a_{n_k}\le\lim_{k\to\infty}\sup A_k\\
  \therefore&&\varlimsup_{k\to\infty}a_k-\lim_{k\to\infty}1/k&\le\lim_{k\to\infty}a_{n_k}\le\varlimsup_{k\to\infty}a_k\\
  \therefore&&\overline\alpha-0=\overline\alpha&\le\alpha\le\overline\alpha
\end{align*}
$$

- $\therefore a_{n_k}\to\overline\alpha$ by squeeze, thus $\overline\alpha\in S\enspace\square$

</details>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** assume ④ , wts ① </summary>

wts $ \forall\varepsilon\gt0\quad\exists\alpha\in A_>\quad\sup S\le\alpha\lt\sup S+\varepsilon $

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

let $\alpha=\sup S+\varepsilon/2$

BWOC assume $|A_{\gt\alpha}|\ge\aleph_0$

$\because A_{\gt\alpha}\subseteq A$ is bounded

$\therefore A_{\gt\alpha}$ has some subsequence that converges to some $\alpha^*\ge\alpha$<span class="float-right">(BW)</span>

$\therefore\alpha^*\in S$ where $\sup S\lt\alpha\le\alpha^*$, thus contradicts that $\sup S$ is upper bound

thus $|A_{\gt\alpha}|\lt\aleph_0$, that is, $\alpha\in A_\gt\enspace\square$
</details>

</details>


<details class="example" open>
<summary>subsequentially converge to any point between liminf and limsup</summary>

for any bounded $(a_n)\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ where

- $\underline{a_n}\to\underline\alpha$ and $\overline{a_n}\to\overline\alpha$

- $|a_n-a_{n+1}|\to0$

<div class="flex justify-center">

$(\underline\alpha,\overline\alpha)$ are subsequential limits of $(a_n)$
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

let subsequences $(a_{\underline n_k})$ and $(a_{\overline n_k})$ such that

$$
\begin{align*}
  a_{\underline n_k}&\to \underline\alpha\\
  a_{\overline n_k}&\to \overline\alpha\\
\end{align*}
$$

let any $\alpha\in(\underline\alpha,\overline\alpha)$

$\therefore\exists N_1\exists N_2$ such that

$$
\forall k\gt N_1,N_2\quad a_{\underline n_k}\lt\alpha\lt a_{\overline n_k}
$$

let any $\varepsilon\gt0$

$\therefore\exists N_3$ such that 

$$
\forall n\gt N_3\quad |a_n-a_{n+1}|\lt\varepsilon
$$

let $N=\max\{N_1,N_2,N_3\}$

let any $k\gt N$

- choose the smallest $\overline n_k$ and smallest $\underline n_k$ such that

$$
k\le\overline n_k\le\underline n_k
$$

- consider $I=\{\overline n_k,\overline n_k+1,\cdots ,\underline n_k\}$ where $a_{\underline n_k}\lt\alpha\lt a_{\overline n_k}$

- $\therefore\exists n_k\in I$ such that $a_{n_k+1}\le\alpha\le a_{n_k}$

$$
\therefore |a_{n_k}-\alpha|=a_{n_k}-\alpha\lt a_{n_k}-a_{n_k+1}=|a_{n_k}-a_{n_k+1}|\lt\varepsilon
$$

thus $a_{n_k}\to \alpha\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary></summary>

for any convergent $(a_n)$ with positive limit point and bounded $(b_n)$

$$
\varlimsup_{n\to\infty}a_nb_n=\left(\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n\right)\left(\varlimsup_{n\to\infty}b_n\right)
$$

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

let $a_n\to \alpha\gt0\quad \overline{b_n}\to \overline\beta\quad \overline{a_nb_n}\to \gamma$

wts $\alpha\overline\beta\le\gamma$

- $\because\exists(b_{n_k})\subseteq(b_n)$ such that $b_{n_k}\to \overline\beta$ where $a_{n_k}\to \alpha$ (under same indices)
- $\therefore a_{n_k}b_{n_k}\to \alpha\overline\beta$, thus $\alpha\overline\beta$ is a subsequential limit (where $\gamma$ is the supremum)

wts $\gamma\le\alpha\overline\beta$

- $\because\exists(a_{n_k}b_{n_k})$ such that $a_{n_k}b_{n_k}\to \gamma$ where 
  - $a_{n_k}\to \alpha$<span class="float-right">(under same indices)</span>
  - $b_{n_k}\to \gamma/\alpha\le\overline\beta$ <span class="float-right">($\overline\beta$ is supremum of subsequential limits)</span>

  $$
  \therefore\gamma=\alpha(\gamma/\alpha)\le \alpha\overline\beta
  $$

thus $\gamma=\alpha\overline\beta\enspace\square$
</details>
</details>

## Series Convergence Tests
<details class="definition" open>
<summary>convergent series</summary>

for series $\sum a_n$

$$
\sum a_n=s\iff s_n\to s
$$

- where $s_n=\sum_{k=1}^n a_k$

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>comparasion test (CT)</summary>

for sequence $(a_n)$ and $(b_n)$ where

$$
0\lt a_n\le b_n
$$

then $\sum b_n\lt\infty\implies \sum a_n\lt\infty$

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>limit comparasion tests</summary>

for sequence $(a_n),(b_n)\subset\mathbb{R}^+$

① if $a_n/b_n\to \alpha\in(0,\infty)$, then

$$
\sum a_n\lt\infty\iff\sum b_n\lt\infty
$$

② if $a_n/b_n\to 0$, then

$$
\sum b_n\lt\infty\implies \sum a_n\lt\infty
$$

③ if $a_n/b_n\to \infty$, then

$$
\sum a_n\lt\infty\implies \sum b_n\lt\infty
$$

</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>absolute convergence</summary>

$\sum a_n$ **absolutely converges** iff

$$
\sum|a_n|\lt\infty
$$

notice AC $\implies $ C
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>root test</summary>

for sequence $(a_n)$

if 

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}|a_{n+1}/a_n|\lt1
$$

then $\sum a_n$ AC

</details>

<summary>geometric series</summary>

$$
\sum_{n\in\omega}ar^n\lt\infty \iff|r|\lt1
$$


if converges, then $\sum ar^n=a/(1-r)$

</details>

<summary>integral test</summary>

for decreasing $f:[N,\infty)\to \mathbb{R}$

$$
\sum_{n=N}^\infty f(n)\lt\infty \iff\int_N^\infty f(x)dx\lt\infty
$$
